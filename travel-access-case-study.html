Overview

Travel Access is a mobile app designed to make trip planning simpler and less stressful. Users can search for flights, manage lodging, and most importantly — build and manage packing checklists for their trips, all in one centralized platform.

As Product Manager on the Co.Lab team, I led the product from discovery through to a shipped MVP — conducting user research, defining the problem space, prioritizing the MVP scope, writing user stories, managing sprints in Jira, and collaborating with a global cross-functional team of designers and engineers.

02

The Problem

Problem Statement: Travellers need a way to keep track of their documentation because it is inconvenient to search for it when needed. Users need a way to search for their information — from booking a flight to hotel details — in a central place, to create a seamless experience.

Vision: The Travel Access team is looking to find ways to add value and make travel easier for its users — developing a web application that will stand out as a go-to travel resource for any upcoming trip.

01

Fragmented Experience

Users were juggling multiple apps and platforms to plan a single trip — flights, lodging, activities, and packing all lived in different places.

02

Hidden Fee Confusion

Major airlines' basic economy fares often included surprise charges for seat selection and carry-ons — users didn't know what they were actually paying for.

03

No Packing System

From 31 survey responses, a recurring theme emerged: travelers needed a reliable checklist system to ensure they didn't forget essentials before departure.

Design Iterations

Based on usability testing, we identified 3 critical issues and made targeted design changes to address each one. Here's what we found and what we changed.

Issue #1 — Login State Confusion

Users couldn't tell if they were logged in

The profile icon in the navigation gave no clear signal about authentication state — users were unsure whether they had an active session.

Before
  • Generic profile icon in the nav — same whether logged in or out
  • No visual indication of authentication state
  • Users left guessing about their session
Fix Applied
  • Replaced icon with "Sign In" text when logged out
  • After login: profile photo + welcome message appear
  • Clear visual confirmation of authenticated state

Issue #2 — Confusing Search & Filter

Users didn't know what they were searching for

A generic search bar on the Travel Prep page didn't clarify whether users were searching their own checklists or the platform's templates — causing confusion and abandonment.

Before
  • Generic search bar with a filter icon
  • No distinction between user checklists and site templates
  • "I'm not sure what I will be searching" — user quote
Fix Applied
  • Replaced search bar with a dropdown menu
  • Options: "My Checklists" vs platform templates
  • Explicit categorization removes ambiguity entirely
Issue #3 — Ownership Confusion on Checklists

Users couldn't tell which checklists were theirs

On the Travel Prep screen, city-based checklist templates looked identical to the user's own saved checklists — there was no visual distinction between the two.

Before
  • All checklist cards looked identical
  • No indicator to separate saved vs. template cards
  • "How do I see my saved ones vs blank templates?"
Fix Applied
  • Added a heart icon on saved/favorited checklists
  • Instantly distinguishes personal saves from templates
  • Familiar pattern — mirrors how most apps handle saves

The Solution

After research and iteration, we focused the MVP on the packing checklist feature — the single most requested capability from user research. This gave us a tight, shippable scope while delivering immediate value.

✈️

Flight Search

Users can search for round-trip or one-way flights, select traveler count and ticket class — powered by the Amadeus API.

📋

Packing Checklist (MVP Focus)

Destination-based checklist templates users can save, customize, and share with travel companions — addressing the #1 user need from research.

🗺️

Explore Destinations

A discover section surfaces destination inspiration and key travel information to help users plan with context.

08

Reflection

Travel Access was my first full product lifecycle experience — from problem discovery through shipped MVP. Here's what I'd do differently and what I'd carry into every product I build.

My PM Learnings

I learned how to interact with a designer and developers in a real product environment. I applied theories of product management and learned to write user stories and acceptance criteria. I managed sprints within Jira and got a deeper understanding of how APIs work by collaborating closely with the dev team.

What I'd Do Differently

I'd invest more time in the onboarding flow earlier — user confusion around login state could have been caught in wireframe reviews rather than usability testing. Earlier design critiques with the team would have surfaced this sooner and saved iteration cycles.

Cross-Functional Leadership

Our team was spread across the USA, Africa, and the Middle East. Navigating time zone differences made async communication and intentional sync meetings essential. This process allowed us to quickly build trust and consistently meet deliverables across borders.

Technical Challenges & Growth

The hardest technical challenge was structuring the database. Working through this with the dev team gave me a much stronger foundation in backend architecture. As our developer Ijeoma put it: "two heads are better than one — working together makes things faster and you learn more from each other."
